Abstract

Multilingual Retrieval-Augmented Generation (mRAG) systems often exhibit a perceived preference for high-resource languages—particularly English—resulting in the widespread adoption of English pivoting. While prior studies attribute this advantage to the superior English-centric capabilities of LLMs, we find that such measurements are significantly distorted by structural priors inherent in evaluation benchmarks.

Specifically, we identify exposure bias and a gold availability prior—both driven by the disproportionate concentration of resources in English—as well as cultural priors rooted in topic locality, as factors that hinder accurate assessment of genuine language preference.

To address these biases, we propose DeLP (Debiased Language Preference), a calibrated metric that explicitly factors out these structural confounds. Our analysis reveals that retrievers fundamentally favor monolingual alignment between the query and document language. Building on this insight, we introduce DELTA (DEbiased Language preference–guided Text Augmentation), a lightweight and efficient mRAG framework that consistently outperforms English pivoting and mRAG baselines across diverse languages.

🔍 The Myth of English Preference

English pivoting—translating non-English queries into English before retrieval—is a dominant mRAG strategy. Prior work attributes this advantage to English-centric LLM capabilities. We challenge this assumption and show that the gains stem from retrieval-side structural biases, not intrinsic model preference.

📊 Exposure Bias

High-resource corpora (especially English) dominate the top retrieval results regardless of the encoder's linguistic intent—a "popularity bias" of the datastore that artificially inflates English performance.

📌 Gold Availability Prior

For a vast majority of benchmark queries, English Wikipedia is the sole repository of ground-truth information. Retrieval is structurally forced into English—not because of model preference, but because local-language gold simply doesn't exist.

🌏 Cultural Prior

Locale-tied queries contain native surface forms (titles, aliases, scripts) that act as retrieval anchors. A language may appear "preferred" simply because the benchmark overrepresents topics from that locale—not due to intrinsic model tendency.

Key Finding: On MKQA, for non-English languages like Arabic and Korean, over 65% of gold passages exist only in English Wikipedia. English pivoting works not because of linguistic superiority—it's because the evidence only exists in English.

📐 DeLP: Debiased Language Preference

To reveal the intrinsic preference of mRAG systems, we propose DeLP—a calibrated measurement that explicitly regresses out structural confounds via ridge regression. DeLP treats the residual signal as the true, debiased preference of the model.

How DeLP Works

For each language pair \((L_q, L_d)\), we construct a prior feature vector \(\phi(L_q, L_d)\) stacking interpretable covariates:

  • \(p_\text{ret}(L_d \mid L_q)\) — exposure prior (log-transformed)
  • \(p_\text{db}(L_d)\) — corpus-size prior
  • \(\ell(L_d)\) — passage-length statistic
  • \(p_\text{gold}(L_q, L_d)\) — gold-availability prior
  • \(p_\text{cult}(L_d)\) — cultural prior
  • \(\mathbb{I}[L_q = L_d]\) — monolingual alignment indicator

We fit a ridge regression per encoder to predict raw scores from priors, then use the residual as the debiased preference: \[ \mathrm{DeLP}_e(L_q, L_d) = s_e(L_q, L_d) - \phi(L_q, L_d)^\top \hat\beta_e + \mu_e \]

🎯 Emergence of Monolingual Alignment: After calibration, the strongest signal consistently moves to the diagonal (\(L_q = L_d\)). The previously dominant English preference largely disappears — revealing that retrievers fundamentally favor monolingual alignment. Much of the apparent English preference was an artifact of structural priors.

We validate DeLP by computing correlations between preference scores and priors before vs. after calibration. Raw scores (MLRS) are highly correlated with all three priors. After applying DeLP, these correlations drop sharply, confirming that DeLP effectively decouples intrinsic model tendencies from structural signals.

⚡ DELTA: Preference-Aligned Query Fusion

Building on the discovery of monolingual alignment, we introduce DELTA (DEbiased Language preference–guided Text Augmentation)—a lightweight, query-level mRAG solution. DELTA fuses global and local cues into a single preference-aligned query with no corpus or retriever modifications.

Fused Query Structure

Given a local query \(q_\text{local}\), DELTA constructs a fused query \(Q_\text{fused}\) from five segments:

[GLOB] — English pivot query \(q_\text{glob}\) for broad coverage
[LOCAL] — Original native-language query to leverage monolingual alignment
[TITLE_BRIDGE] — Paired canonical titles \((t_\text{glob}, t_\text{loc})\) for cross-lingual entity mapping
[ALIASES] — Global/local aliases as stable retrieval anchors
[LOCALE_HINT] — Region/disambiguation hint

Repetition-Based Weighting

DELTA infers a cultural cue \((y, c, L_\text{loc})\)—whether the query is culture-specific, confidence score, and locale language—then dynamically adjusts segment repetition:

  • Culture-specific + high confidence → upweight [LOCAL] (up to 3×) to preserve native surface-form anchors
  • Non-culture-specific → mildly favor [GLOB] (2×) for robust English back-off
  • High-confidence culture-specific queries also duplicate [TITLE_BRIDGE] and [ALIASES] for stronger entity grounding
